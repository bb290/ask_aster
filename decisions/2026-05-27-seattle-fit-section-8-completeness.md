# Decision — Seattle First-In-Time and Section 8: Completeness, Not Voucher Status, Sets Order

## Decision

Inside the City of Seattle, the First-In-Time (FIT) rule (SMC 14.08.050) requires
that we offer tenancy to the first applicant who meets our screening criteria and
submits a complete application. This is a legal requirement, not a Sagareus
preference. Source of income is also protected (RCW 59.18.255, SMC 14.08). We
never rank an applicant lower because they use a Section 8 voucher. We rank by
completeness and order of application, neutral criteria applied identically to
everyone.

## Scope note

FIT applies inside the City of Seattle. Source-of-income protection under RCW
59.18.255 applies statewide. When a unit sits inside Seattle, apply FIT.

## The core tension

Two rules collide for Section 8 applicants:

1. First-In-Time says the first complete qualifying application wins the unit.
2. Section 8 adds required steps, Request for Tenancy Approval (RFTA), housing
   authority approval, and inspection, that take 3 to 4 weeks to clear.

The result: a Section 8 applicant can be a strong, qualified prospect and still
not be first in line, because their application is not yet complete. Completeness,
not voucher status, is what determines order. This is the lawful distinction that
keeps FIT and source-of-income protection compatible, and it is the one leasing
agents need to understand cold.

## What this means in practice

- A Section 8 applicant's status is typically "Conditionally Approved, Pending
  Section 8." They have met our screening criteria, but the file is not complete
  until the Section 8 steps clear.
- Because the application is not complete, they are not first in line, and per the
  overview policy, the Leasing Team continues showing and actively working to
  lease the property while the Section 8 steps are in progress.
- This means the unit may be leased to another applicant who submits a complete
  application first. That is a lawful FIT outcome, not a denial based on source of
  income.
- We do not hold the property for a Section 8 applicant, or any applicant, unless
  the property owner specifically instructs us to.

## Why this disadvantages Section 8 applicants

The disadvantage is real, and it is structural rather than discriminatory. The
voucher process simply takes longer than a conventional application, so on a
high-demand or actively-marketed unit, a Section 8 applicant frequently loses out
to a faster, complete application. Leasing agents find this genuinely difficult.
The applicant is qualified and we want to house them, but FIT does not let us
reorder applicants to compensate, and doing so would itself create legal exposure.

## How to actually help Section 8 applicants

We cannot bend the FIT rule, but we can change the timeline so the applicant's
file is complete sooner, ideally complete first. This works, and we have done it
successfully on multiple occasions.

1. Start the process early. The moment a Section 8 prospect expresses interest,
   begin moving on the steps that take time. Do not wait for the rest of the file.
2. Match them to the right properties. Steer applicants toward units that meet
   their voucher criteria (bedroom size, rent within the payment standard, areas
   where their authority operates). A unit that fits avoids delays from RFTA or
   inspection problems down the line.
3. Target the right inventory. Section 8 applicants fare best on units that are
   newly available or lower-demand, where the timeline to a complete competing
   application is longer. On a hot unit, the math rarely works in their favor, so
   set expectations honestly and early.
4. Get them first in line. The goal of starting early is simple: complete their
   application, RFTA submitted and paperwork in, before competing complete
   applications arrive. If their file is the first complete one, FIT works for them.
5. Keep the file moving. Treat every Section 8 step as time-sensitive. Be the
   squeaky wheel with the housing authority so RFTA approval and inspection
   scheduling do not stall.
