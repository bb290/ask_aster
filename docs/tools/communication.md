# Stakeholders & Communication Decorum

Effective communication requires clarity, intention, and respect for the audience. All communication must be directed only to the relevant stakeholder(s) and conducted with appropriate decorum at all times.

Sending information to the wrong stakeholder or to too many stakeholders creates unnecessary confusion, conflict, and inefficiency. As the saying goes: *too many cooks in the kitchen* (and somehow no one salted the soup).


:::warning
Failure to follow these principles increases the risk of misalignment, escalation, and reputational harm:

:::

**Right audience, right message.** Communicate only with those who need the information to act, decide, or remain informed.

**Minimum necessary participation.** Always limit communication to the smallest group required.

**Actively manage threads.** Remove individuals from email or message threads once information is no longer relevant to them.

**Assume messages may be forwarded or reviewed later.** Write every message as if it could be read by leadership, legal counsel, or a third party.


---

## Stakeholder Categories

Before sending any communication, identify **who** you are communicating with and **why**.

### Internal

* **Sagareus Team**
  * Internal coordination, documentation, task ownership, and decision-making
  * Primary platform: Asana (email only when necessary)

### Clients

* **Current Clients (property owners, owners)**
  * Financial matters, approvals, strategy, asset performance
* **Potential Clients**
  * Terms & pricing, scope of work, clear expectations

### Residents

* **Current Residents**
  * Lease matters, maintenance, notices, habitability issues
* **Potential Residents (Applicants)**
  * Screening, application status, next steps, adverse action

### Third-Party Vendors

* **Maintenance Vendors**
* **Utility Vendors**
* **Appraisers**
  * Scope of work, scheduling, access, and technical coordination only
  * Never include clients or residents unless explicitly required

### Third-Party Authority Figures

* **Police / Fire**
* **City Inspectors / City Personnel**
* **Housing Authority Personnel**
  * Communication must be factual, professional, concise, and compliant
  * Never speculative, emotional, or casual

## Decorum & Tone Standards

Decorum refers to how we communicate not just what we communicate. All communication must be tailored to the audience, with tone, language, and level of detail appropriate to the stakeholder receiving the message.

### Audience-Appropriate Tone

Different stakeholders require different communication styles. Adjust tone and language accordingly:

* **Property Owners (Clients)**
  * Generally more educated and financially literate
  * Expect clear explanations, rationale, and outcomes
  * Professional, confident, and solution-oriented tone
  * Avoid jargon, but do not oversimplify critical details
* **Residents & Applicants**
  * Wide range of education levels and lived experiences
  * English as a second language is common
  * Use plain, direct language with short sentences
  * Avoid legal jargon, idioms, sarcasm, or "clever" phrasing
  * Focus on *what is happening*, *what is required*, and *what happens next*
* **Vendors (Maintenance, Utilities, Appraisers)**
  * Action-driven communication
  * Clear scope, deadlines, access details, and expectations
  * No unnecessary background, opinions, or commentary
  * Keep messages concise and task-focused
* **Authority Figures (Housing Authorities, City Staff, Inspectors, Emergency Services)**
  * Factual, neutral, and precise
  * No speculation, emotion, or assumptions
  * Documented, accurate, and compliant language only


---

### Plain English Standard

Sagareus defaults to **simple, plain English** across all communications.

* Avoid "$10 words" when a $1 word is clearer
* Complex vocabulary can sound condescending, especially to non-native speakers
* Clarity is a sign of competence; complexity is not


:::info
**Example:**

* Don't: "Please remit the outstanding balance at your earliest convenience."
* Do: "Please submit payment for the remaining balance by \[date\]."

If a message needs to be reread to understand it, it needs to be rewritten.

:::

#### Professional is not Cold

Professional communication does **not** mean robotic or unfriendly.

* Be polite, calm, and respectful
* Avoid emotional language, defensiveness, or moral judgment
* Do not lecture, scold, or threaten
* Never shame a resident, vendor, or client, even when enforcing policy

Firm, clear boundaries can (and should) coexist with a respectful tone.


---

### Permanent and Shareable


:::warning
All written communication should be treated as **permanent and shareable**.

Assume messages may be:

* Forwarded
* Screen-captured
* Reviewed by attorneys, regulators, or courts

Never include:

* Personal opinions
* Frustration or sarcasm
* Speculation or blame
* Internal process commentary

If it would sound bad read aloud in a deposition, don't send it.

:::


---

### When in Doubt


:::tip
Before sending, ask:

* Is this the correct stakeholder?
* Is the language clear to someone with English as a second language?
* Does this message move the situation forward?
* Would I be comfortable seeing this quoted out of context?

If the answer isn't a confident yes, revise.

:::
